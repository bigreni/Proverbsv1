  • "A Dios rogando y con el mazo dando."

    Literal translation: “Praying to God and hitting with the hammer.” Covering all the bases. Doing everything necessary to ensure success.

  • "A juventud ociosa, vejez trabajosa."

    Literal translation: “To leisurely youth, laborious old age.” If you are lazy now, you will have to work harder later.

  • "A quien madruga, Dios le ayuda."

    Literal translation: “Who rises early, God helps.” God helps those who get started early.

  • "Acabándose el dinero, se termina la amistad."

    Literal translation: “The money running out, the friendship ends.” Describes those who are your “friends” only as long as you have money.

  • "Camarón que se duerme, se lo lleva la corriente."

    Literal translation: “Shrimp that sleeps, the current carries it away.” If you don’t stay alert to and act on opportunities, you will miss them. Similar to the English, “Snooze and you lose.

  • "Como el apóstol 13, come y desaparece."

    Literal translation: “Like the apostle 13, eats and leaves.” Describes guests who come only for the food, or people who stay only for the part of the event that benefits them.

  • "Con virtud y bondad se adquiere autoridad."

    Literal translation: “With virtue and goodness authority is acquired.” People will be more likely to do what you say if they perceive you as being virtuous and kind.

  • "De dinero y bondad, siempre la mitad."

    Literal translation: “Of money and goodness, always in the middle.” Your interests are better served by being motivated equally by goodness and money, rather than being motivated completely by one or the other.

  • "De tal palo, tal astilla."

    Literal translation: “From such a stick, such a splinter.” The way a child behaves is a reflection of the way his or her parents behave. Similar to “The apple doesn’t fall far from the tree.

  • "Del dicho al hecho, hay mucho trecho."

    Literal translation: “From the word to the deed, there is a great distance.” It is one thing to say something will be done, and quite a different thing to get it done.

  • "Despacio voy, porque de prisa estoy."

    Literal translation: “Slowly I go because I am in a hurry.” Proceeding methodically often gets faster and better results than rushing.

  • "Donde hay gana, hay maña."

    Literal translation: “Where there is the desire there is the ability.” If you really want to do something, you can find a way to do it. Similar to “Where there’s a will, there’s a way.” in English.

  • "El que nace pa’ tamal, del cielo le caen las hojas."

    Literal translation: “He who is born for tamal, from the sky the leaves fall on him.” For what you are born to do, your path will fall into place in front of you.

  • "El mal escribano le echa la culpa a la pluma."

    Literal translation: "The poor writer places the blame on the pen." People naturally blame their problems on something or someone else, rather than accept any responsibility themselves.

  • "El que da primero, da dos veces."

    Literal translation: "He who strikes first strikes twice. Whoever is first has an advantage over all who come after.

  • "El que mucho habla, mucho yerra."

    Literal translation: "Who much speaks, much errs." The more you talk, the more you will make mistakes.

  • "El que por su gusto corre, nunca se cansa."

    Literal translation: "Who for his pleasure runs, never tires." When you do something for pleasure, it is not tiring.

  • "El que quiere baile, que pague músico."

    Literal translation: "Who wants dance, should pay musician." The one who wants something done should be the one who takes the responsibility for making it happen.

  • "El que quita la ocasión, evita el ladrón."

    Literal translation: "Who takes away the opportunity, avoids the robber." If you take precautions, you will avoid problems.

  • "En boca del mentiroso, lo cierto se hace dudoso."

    Literal translation: "In the mouth of a liar, what is certain becomes doubtful." Once someone has been caught lying, it is hard to believe anything else that person says.

  • "Gato escaldo del agua fría huye."

    Literal translation: "A scalded cat from cold water runs." People often draw the overly broad lessons from their experiences. (i.e., The cat should have learned only to avoid hot water.)

  • "Hazme las cuentas claras, y el chocolate espeso."

    Literal translation: "Make for me the accounts clear and the chocolate thick." Whatever else might be confusing, the books had better be straightforward. A similar saying is “Las cuentas claras hacen buenos amigos.” (Clear accounting makes good friends.)

  • "Honra y dinero se ganan despacio y se pierden ligero."

    Literal translation: "Reputation and money are earned slowly and lost quickly." Reputation and money are hard to earn and easy to lose.

  • "La mejor palabra es la que no se dice"

    Literal translation: "The best word is the one that is not said." Sometimes, refraining from speaking is better than anything you could say.

  • "La palabra es plata, el silencio oro."

    Literal translation: "The word is silver, silence gold." Silence is more valuable than words.

  • "Lo que bien se aprende, nunca se pierde."

    Literal translation: "What well is learned never is lost." If you learn something well, you will never forget it.

  • "Más vale poco y bueno que mucho y malo."

    Literal translation: "It is worth more little and good than much and bad." More is not always better. It is better to have less and happiness than more and misery.

  • "Mejor solo que mal acompañado."

    Literal translation: "Better alone than poorly accompanied." It is better to be alone than to be with the wrong person.

  • "Nadie es profeta en su propia tierra."

    Literal translation: "No one is a prophet in his own land." People place a higher value on exotic things and exotic people than on familiar ones or ones from close to home.

  • "No es más rico el que más tiene, sino el que menos necesita.

    Literal translation: "He is not richer who the most has, but who the least needs." Someone who is satisfied with a small amount is richer than someone who is always craving more.

  • "No hay atajo sin trabajo."

    Literal translation: "There is no shortcut without work." It takes work to avoid doing work.

  • "No hay curva mala pasándola despacio."

    Literal translation: "There is no bad curve, passing it slowly." You can avoid mishaps by proceeding with caution.

  • "No tengas como vano el consejo del anciano."

    Literal translation: "Do not consider useless the advice of an old person." Do not ignore the advice of someone who speaks from experience.

  • "Perro que no camina, no encuentra hueso."

    Literal translation: "Dog who doesn’t walk, doesn’t find a bone." If you want something, you need to make efforts to get it.

  • "Poco a poco se anda lejos."

    Literal translation: "Little by little one goes far." Frequent, small steps can accomplish the same result as (or better results than) a one-time Herculean effort.

  • "Por el árbol se conoce el fruto."

    Literal translation: "By the tree the fruit is known." Children are very much like their parents.

  • "Querer es poder."

    Literal translation: "To want to is to be able to." If you really want to do something, you will be able to do it.

  • "Quien con el lobo se junta a aullar aprende."

    Literal translation: "Who with the wolf associates, to howl learns." A person can be judged by the company he or she keeps (i.e., by the people he or she hangs out with).

  • "Si quieres el perro, acepta las pulgas."

    Literal translation: "If you want the dog, accept the fleas." If you wanted something, don’t complain about it once you get it. Similar to the English saying “You have to take the bitter with the sweet.”

  • "Una buena acción es la mejor oración."

    Literal translation: "A good deed is the best prayer." One’s faith is best expressed by one’s actions.
